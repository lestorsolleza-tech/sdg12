Water Conservation & Responsible Use

Water conservation is essential to Responsible Consumption and Production (SDG 12). Every product we use requires water at some stage of its production. Unsustainable water use leads to scarcity, pollution, ecosystem damage, and economic loss.

2024 Global Update: As of 2024, 2.1 billion people (26% of the global population) still lacked safely managed drinking water services, including 106 million who drink directly from surface water sources. Global coverage improved from 68% in 2015 to 74% in 2024.
Source: WHO/UNICEF Joint Monitoring Programme (JMP) Report 2025, published August 26, 2025 | View Report →

Why Water Conservation Matters

Water is fundamental to all life and economic activity. Conserving water ensures availability for households, agriculture, industry, and future generations. Despite progress, billions still lack access to safe water.

  • Protects freshwater ecosystems and biodiversity
  • Reduces energy demand for water treatment and distribution
  • Supports food security through sustainable agriculture
  • Prevents water-related diseases that affect millions
  • Ensures equitable access for all communities
Local Research Finding: Studies near WVSU Janiuay Campus show that 73% of households are aware of water-saving practices, and 61% actively implement them by fixing leaks and minimizing water use during cleaning. However, many report difficulty adopting conservation habits due to lack of awareness or inconsistent household routines.

Practical Ways to Preserve Water

Households

Fix leaks immediately, turn off taps when not needed, install water-efficient fixtures (low-flow showerheads, dual-flush toilets), and use appliances fully loaded to reduce household water waste.

Agriculture

Drip irrigation, soil-moisture monitoring, and rainwater harvesting reduce water waste while maintaining or improving crop yields. Precision agriculture optimizes water use.

Industry

Closed-loop water systems, wastewater treatment and recycling, and process optimization minimize freshwater extraction and reduce pollution from industrial activities.

Insights from International Agencies

  • WHO emphasizes water efficiency as a key factor in preventing disease, reducing healthcare costs, and improving overall public health.
  • The World Health Organization sets standards and guidelines for drinking-water quality, and provides technical support to countries to improve water treatment and management.
  • WHO promotes access to sanitation facilities, and advocates for handwashing with soap and water to prevent the spread of diseases.
  • The organization addresses the impact of climate change on water security and public health, including the increased risk of water scarcity and water-related disasters.
  • 2024 Finding: 1 in 4 people globally still lack access to safe drinking water, with significant disparities between urban and rural areas.

Source: WHO Water & Sanitation

  • Climate-driven rainfall changes increase droughts and floods, making sustainable water use essential for resilience.
  • UNESCO supports research and development of innovative solutions for water management, including technologies for water conservation and efficient use.
  • The organization promotes education and awareness-raising on climate change and water issues, including the importance of water conservation and sustainable management.
  • Through its Intergovernmental Hydrological Programme, UNESCO fosters international cooperation on water issues, including the management of transboundary water resources.

Source: UNESCO Water Programme

  • Water efficiency investments generate long-term economic benefits and reduce future infrastructure costs.
  • The World Bank estimates that climate change could push an additional 100 million people into poverty by 2030, with water scarcity and decreased agricultural productivity being major factors.
  • Water scarcity could lead to a 6% decline in global GDP by 2050, with the most vulnerable populations being those in low-income countries.
  • Investing in water management and climate resilience can have significant economic benefits, with every dollar invested generating an estimated $4 in economic returns.
